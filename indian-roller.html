Indian Roller

Coracias benghalensis

Weight 90–120 g
Length 30–34 cm
Wingspan ~65 cm
Sri Lanka · South Asia · Middle East

Few birds command the eye quite like the Indian Roller. Perched on a wire or bare branch it appears an unremarkable brown — but the instant it takes flight, wings of blazing turquoise, cobalt and violet erupt into the sky, earning it a sacred place in Indian mythology and the status of state bird of three Indian states. In Sri Lanka it is a conspicuous, year-round resident of the open dry zone.

Field Notes
🌿
Habitat
Open Country & Dry Woodland
Open scrub, dry deciduous woodland, cultivated plains, paddy field margins, grasslands and roadside trees. Strongly favours open landscapes with scattered tall perches — telephone wires, fence posts and bare dead branches overlooking low vegetation.
📍
Distribution
Middle East to Southeast Asia
Resident from Iraq and the Arabian Peninsula east through the Indian subcontinent to Myanmar and Thailand. In Sri Lanka it is a common year-round resident of the dry zone lowlands, intermediate zone and the northern plains; scarce in the wet zone.
⚖️
Weight & Size
90–120 g · 30–34 cm
A stocky, broad-winged bird with a large square head, heavy hooked bill and a moderately long square-tipped tail. Noticeably larger than a Common Myna. The wings appear broad and rounded in flight, perfectly suited to the spectacular aerial rolling displays.
🍃
Diet
Insects, Lizards & Small Frogs
Large ground insects — grasshoppers, beetles, scorpions, centipedes — as well as lizards, small frogs, crabs and occasionally small snakes. Drops to the ground from a perch to seize prey, then returns to the same vantage point to feed.
🔊
Voice
Harsh chak · kraaak
A loud, harsh, crow-like "chak" or "kraak-kraak" — raucous and far-carrying. Also produces a series of loud screeches during aerial display flights. Generally a noisy species, calling frequently from its perch and in flight.
🟢
Conservation
Least Concern
IUCN Red List — Least Concern. Widespread and common across its large range. State bird of Odisha, Karnataka and Telangana in India, where it holds cultural and religious significance. Locally at risk from pesticide use reducing insect prey.
About this Species

Appearance

The Indian Roller (Coracias benghalensis) is one of South Asia's most deceptive birds — unremarkable when perched, utterly spectacular in flight. At rest the plumage appears predominantly brown: the crown and nape are a dull olive-brown to rufous-brown, the mantle and back a warm earthy brown, and the underparts a lilac-washed buff with fine white streaking on the throat and upper breast that catches the light at close range. The face and lores show a pale buffish wash and the heavy, slightly hooked bill is dark grey-brown. Only the wings hint at what lies beneath — with a dull blue shoulder visible even at rest.

In flight, the transformation is extraordinary. The wings open to reveal a dazzling mosaic of turquoise, cobalt-blue, violet-purple and deep ultramarine — brilliant colours that flash and shift as the bird banks and turns. The outer primaries are a deep, rich cobalt-blue; the inner primaries and coverts a bright turquoise-green; and a band of deep violet-purple runs across the middle of the wing between the two. The rump and uppertail-coverts are a vivid pale blue. Both sexes are identical in plumage — the species shows no sexual dimorphism — and immatures are a duller version of the adults, with browner tones and less saturated wing colours that intensify with each successive moult.

Behaviour & Feeding

The Indian Roller is a sit-and-wait ground hunter that operates from a prominent elevated perch — a telephone wire, a bare branch, a fencepost or a tall rock — from which it surveys the open ground beneath for prey. When a large insect, lizard or small frog is spotted, it drops in a fast, direct plunge, seizes the prey with its heavy bill and returns to the same perch to beat it against the branch before swallowing it whole, head-first. Unlike bee-eaters it rarely pursues aerial prey; almost all hunting is directed at terrestrial targets on or near the ground.

The species is named for its spectacular aerial courtship display — a series of wild, rocking side-to-side rolls and swooping dives performed at height by the male, accompanied by loud screeching calls, that expose the full brilliance of the wing colours to the watching female below. This rolling flight is the most dramatic and distinctive behaviour of the family Coraciidae and gives all rollers their common name. In Sri Lanka the Indian Roller is a bird of the dry zone plains, most abundant around Yala, Wilpattu, Minneriya, Udawalawe and the northern agricultural lowlands, where pairs hold year-round territories along roads, field margins and the edges of scrub forest.

Breeding

Breeding in Sri Lanka occurs mainly between March and June. The nest is an unlined cavity in a tree hollow, a crevice in a wall, an earthen bank or an abandoned woodpecker hole — occasionally also in the crown of a coconut palm. No nesting material is added; eggs are laid directly on the bare surface of the cavity. The female lays three to five rounded, glossy white eggs. Both parents share incubation, which lasts 17–19 days. The chicks are altricial — hatching blind and naked — and are fed by both parents on large insects and small vertebrates. They fledge at approximately 28–30 days. Pairs are strongly territorial and frequently perform aerial rolling displays above the nest site to advertise ownership and deter rivals throughout the breeding season.
