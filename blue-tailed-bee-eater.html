Blue-tailed Bee-eater

Merops philippinus

Weight 24–35 g

South & SE Asia · Sri Lanka

A slender, jewel-toned aerial hunter of open skies — the Blue-tailed Bee-eater sweeps and glides over paddy fields, rivers and coastal wetlands with effortless grace. Its brilliant green plumage, chestnut throat, turquoise tail and twin elongated central streamers make it one of Sri Lanka's most elegantly designed migratory visitors.

Field Notes
🌿
Habitat
Open Country & Wetlands
Paddy fields, river banks, coastal lagoons, open scrubland, grasslands and forest clearings. Strongly favours open airspace near sandy or earthen banks suitable for nesting. Often seen perched on telephone wires and bare branches over open ground.
📍
Distribution
South & Southeast Asia
Breeds in India, Sri Lanka, Southeast Asia east to the Philippines. In Sri Lanka it is a seasonal breeding visitor — common in the dry zone from February to September, with large flocks arriving to nest in sandy banks and riverine areas.
⚖️
Weight & Size
24–35 g · 23–26 cm
Slender and long-winged with a fine, decurved bill and two elongated central tail feathers that extend 2–3 cm beyond the tail tip. The pointed wingtips and tapering silhouette are characteristic in flight.
🍃
Diet
Bees, Wasps & Dragonflies
Honey bees, wasps, hornets, dragonflies and other large flying insects. Before swallowing stinging prey, the bird beats it repeatedly against its perch and rubs the abdomen to discharge the venom — a remarkable instinctive behaviour.
🔊
Voice
Rolling prreee-prreee
A distinctive, liquid, rolling "prreee" or "prrit-prrit" — a far-carrying trill delivered in flight, often announcing a passing flock overhead before the birds come into view. Colonies are continuously vocal near the nest.
🟢
Conservation
Least Concern
IUCN Red List — Least Concern. Widespread and locally abundant. Main threats are loss of open sandy riverbanks through flood control and sand mining, which reduce available nesting habitat for colonial breeding populations.
About this Species

Appearance

The Blue-tailed Bee-eater (Merops philippinus) is a slender, brilliantly coloured bee-eater combining multiple vivid tones in a single, harmonious plumage. The crown, nape, mantle, back and scapulars are a rich, bright grass-green, while the rump and uppertail-coverts transition to a luminous turquoise-blue — the feature from which the common name derives. The tail is deep turquoise-blue with two central feathers elongated into fine, pointed wire-like streamers extending 2–3 cm beyond the rest of the tail. In flight, the underwing is a warm rufous-chestnut, creating a striking contrast with the green upperparts as the bird banks and turns.

The face pattern is distinctive and precisely marked: a broad black mask runs from the base of the bill through and behind the eye, bordered above by a narrow pale blue supercilium. The throat is a warm chestnut to rufous-orange, separated from the pale greenish-yellow breast and belly by a narrow black gorget band. The long, slender bill is black and strongly decurved — perfectly designed for snatching flying insects in mid-air. Both sexes are alike in plumage; immatures are duller overall, lack the full elongation of the central tail streamers and show a less vivid chestnut throat.

Behaviour & Feeding

The Blue-tailed Bee-eater is a masterful aerial predator that hunts entirely on the wing. It selects an exposed perch — a bare wire, a dead branch or the top of a low shrub — with an unobstructed view, scanning the airspace around it for flying insects. When prey is spotted it launches into a fast, fluid pursuit, twisting and gliding through the air with remarkable precision before snapping the insect in its bill and returning to the same perch. Larger or stinging prey — bees, wasps and hornets — is first beaten vigorously against the perch and rubbed against the branch tip to discharge the sting and squeeze out the venom sac before being swallowed head-first.

In Sri Lanka the species is most conspicuous in the dry zone lowlands, particularly around the great reservoirs, paddy fields and river systems of the north-central and southern provinces. Large flocks of 50–200 birds are commonly seen hawking insects over paddy fields during the north-east monsoon season. The species is a highly social and gregarious bird, foraging, roosting and nesting in colonies. Flocks wheel and call continuously in the open sky, producing the species' characteristic liquid trilling that is one of the signature sounds of Sri Lanka's dry zone landscape.

Breeding

The Blue-tailed Bee-eater is a colonial ground-nester, breeding in loose to dense colonies of a few pairs to several hundred. In Sri Lanka breeding takes place mainly between February and May. Both sexes excavate a long horizontal tunnel — typically 1–1.5 m deep — in a sandy or earthy bank, a flat sandy field, a road cutting or a sandy river margin, using their bills and feet to loosen and kick out soil. The tunnel ends in a rounded egg chamber. No nesting material is used. The female lays five to seven rounded, glossy white eggs directly on the bare earth. Both parents — and occasionally unmated helper birds — share incubation, which lasts approximately 20–22 days. Chicks are fed on de-stung insects by both adults and fledge after about 25–30 days. Colonies are conspicuous and noisy, with a constant stream of birds arriving and departing the nest bank throughout the day.
