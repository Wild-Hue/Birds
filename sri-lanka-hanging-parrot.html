Sri Lanka Hanging Parrot

Loriculus beryllinus

Weight 28–35 g
Length 13–14 cm
Wingspan ~18 cm
Sri Lanka Endemic

A tiny, acrobatic jewel found only in Sri Lanka — the Hanging Parrot earns its name from its extraordinary habit of roosting and feeding upside-down, bat-like, from the tips of branches. Its vivid emerald-green plumage, scarlet cap and brilliant orange-red rump make it one of the island's most charming and distinctive endemic birds.

Habitat
Forest, Gardens & Plantations
Found across a wide range of wooded habitats — from tall primary rainforest and forest edges to home gardens, coconut and rubber plantations, and fruiting trees in villages and town parks.

Distribution

A strict Sri Lanka endemic, found nowhere else on Earth. Widespread and common from sea level to mid-hills across the wet zone, dry zone and intermediate zone, up to approximately 1,500 m.
⚖️
Weight & Size
28–35 g · 13–14 cm
Sri Lanka's smallest parrot. Compact and short-tailed with a strongly hooked red bill and zygodactyl feet — two toes forward, two back — perfectly adapted for clinging and hanging upside-down.
🍃
Diet
Nectar, Fruits & Flowers
Ripe soft fruits, flower nectar, pollen and seeds. Particularly fond of coconut flower nectar and papaya. Uses its brush-tipped tongue to lap nectar — playing an important pollinator role.
🔊
Voice
Sharp tsit-tsit-tsit
A rapid, high-pitched, metallic "tsit-tsit-tsit" or "chee-chee-chee" — delivered continuously in flight and while foraging. Flocks are very noisy and betray their presence from a distance.
🟢
Conservation

IUCN Red List — Least Concern. One of Sri Lanka's most common and adaptable endemics. Population is stable and benefits from its tolerance of modified habitats including gardens and plantations.
About this Species

Appearance

The Sri Lanka Hanging Parrot (Loriculus beryllinus) is a tiny, brilliantly coloured parrot found only on the island of Sri Lanka. The overall plumage is a vivid emerald-green, somewhat brighter and more saturated on the underparts than the back. The male's most striking feature is his bold scarlet-red crown patch, which covers the forehead and forecrown and contrasts sharply with the surrounding green. The rump and uppertail-coverts are a rich orange-red to flame-orange, visible as a flash of colour when the bird takes flight or hangs from a branch. The throat is washed with a variable patch of pale cobalt-blue — more prominent in some individuals than others, and generally duller or absent in females. The short, square tail is green above and yellowish below. The bill is strongly hooked and deep red, and the eyes are pale yellowish-white, giving the bird a bright, alert expression.

The female differs in being slightly duller overall, with a reduced or absent red crown patch and a less vivid blue throat wash. Immature birds lack the red crown entirely and show a plain green head, acquiring adult plumage gradually over the first year. The species is monotypic — no subspecies are recognised — making it unique among Sri Lanka's endemic parrots.

Behaviour & Feeding

True to its common name, the Sri Lanka Hanging Parrot is famous for its habit of roosting and resting upside-down — hanging beneath a branch with its feet locked around a twig, bat-like, with the body dangling freely below. This behaviour is shared by all members of the genus Loriculus and is thought to aid thermoregulation and provide a degree of camouflage beneath leafy canopies. The bird also frequently feeds in inverted postures, using its feet and hooked bill together to manipulate flowers and fruit.

It is a highly active, fast-moving species that travels in small, noisy flocks of 4–15 birds, often flying high and fast between fruiting or flowering trees. In flight the compact silhouette, rapid wingbeats and continuous metallic calls make it readily identifiable. It is one of the most important pollinators of coconut palms in Sri Lanka, visiting the flower spathes to feed on nectar and inadvertently transferring pollen between trees. It is also regularly attracted to garden plantings of papaya, banana and mango.

Breeding

The Sri Lanka Hanging Parrot breeds throughout much of the year, with peaks between January and May and again in August–September. The nest is a natural tree cavity or an abandoned woodpecker hole, lined with leaves, moss and plant fibres that the female carries to the nest tucked among her rump feathers — a remarkable and unique behaviour shared across the genus. The female lays two to four small, rounded white eggs and handles the majority of incubation, which lasts approximately 20 days. Both parents feed the chicks, which fledge at around 33 days. The species readily nests in garden trees and is one of the few Sri Lankan endemic birds to thrive in close proximity to human habitation.
