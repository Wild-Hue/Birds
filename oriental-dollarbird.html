Oriental Dollarbird

Eurystomus orientalis

Weight 100–130 g
Length 27–30 cm
Wingspan ~48 cm
South Asia · SE Asia · Australia

Named for the pair of pale blue–white circular wing patches that flash like silver coins in flight, the Oriental Dollarbird is a stocky, broad-winged roller of open forest and tall dead perches. In Sri Lanka it is a spectacular seasonal visitor, launching into dramatic aerial pursuits for insects at dusk with its vivid red bill blazing against the darkening sky.

Field Notes
🌿
Habitat
Open Forest & Forest Edges
Forest clearings, forest edges, tall dead trees in open country, plantations and well-wooded gardens. Strongly associated with prominent exposed perches — tall snags, telephone poles and bare treetops — overlooking open airspace for hunting.
📍
Distribution
South Asia to Australia
Breeds across South and Southeast Asia, China, Japan and through to Australia and the Solomon Islands. In Sri Lanka it is primarily a breeding migrant arriving March–April and departing September–October; some individuals may overwinter.
⚖️
Weight & Size
100–130 g · 27–30 cm
Stocky and broad-winged with a large, flat head and a short neck — giving a very front-heavy silhouette in flight. The broad, slightly rounded wings and short square tail are characteristic of the roller family.
🍃
Diet
Aerial Insectivore
Large flying insects — beetles, cicadas, grasshoppers, dragonflies, moths and winged termites. Most active at dawn and dusk; hunts in fast, twisting aerial pursuits, returning to its perch to beat and swallow larger prey.
🔊
Voice
Harsh chak-chak-chak
A loud, raucous, crow-like "chak chak chak" or "kak-kak-kak", delivered repeatedly from a perch or in flight. Noisy and conspicuous near the nest, especially during territorial disputes at dusk.
🟢
Conservation
Least Concern
IUCN Red List — Least Concern. Widespread and locally common across its enormous range from India to Australia. Tolerates modified landscapes well, though dependent on tall mature trees for nesting cavities.
About this Species

Appearance

The Oriental Dollarbird (Eurystomus orientalis) is a thickset, broad-winged roller immediately recognisable by the pair of large, pale blue-white circular patches on the outer primaries — visible as two gleaming "dollar coin" discs when the bird banks in flight, and the origin of its evocative common name. In perched birds these wing patches are concealed, and the overall impression is of a dark, rather uniform bird. The plumage is a deep blue-green to turquoise on the head, breast and underparts, with the mantle and back a dull brownish-black that contrasts with the brighter turquoise of the rump and tail. The throat is a distinctive cobalt-blue, often appearing almost luminous in good light. The short, broad bill is bright coral-red to vermillion with a blackish tip — one of the most striking features of the bird at close range. The legs are short and red.

Both sexes are virtually identical in plumage, making field sexing essentially impossible without behavioural cues. Immature birds are noticeably duller than adults — the blues are replaced by brownish-green tones, and the bill is much darker, becoming redder as the bird approaches adulthood. In flight the broad, somewhat paddle-shaped wings and short square tail give a distinctive silhouette — heavier and blunter than a falcon, with strong, direct wingbeats punctuated by brief glides.

Behaviour & Feeding

The Oriental Dollarbird is a sit-and-wait aerial predator that hunts almost entirely on the wing. It selects a commanding exposed perch — a bare dead branch, a tall snag or a telephone wire with an unobstructed view of open airspace — and waits patiently, scanning for large flying insects. When prey is detected it launches into a fast, powerful pursuit, twisting and rolling through the air with surprising agility for a stocky bird, snapping prey in its wide, flat-based bill before returning to the same perch to swallow it. Hunting activity peaks sharply at dawn and dusk, when cicadas and large beetles take flight in greatest numbers, and on evenings following afternoon storms when termite alates emerge in swarms.

In Sri Lanka the Dollarbird is a conspicuous bird of the lowlands and lower hills, commonly encountered perched on exposed dead branches along forest roads, paddy field margins and the edges of village gardens in both the wet and dry zones. Its loud, raucous calls from a prominent treetop perch are often the first indication of its presence. Pairs tend to remain faithful to the same nesting territory year after year, returning to identical trees each season.

Breeding

In Sri Lanka, breeding occurs between March and July, coinciding with the species' arrival from its wintering grounds. The nest is an unlined natural cavity in a tall tree — a hollow branch, a woodpecker hole or a rotted trunk — typically placed high above the ground with a clear flight path to the entrance. No nesting material is added; the eggs are laid directly on the bare wood of the cavity floor. The female lays three to four rounded, glossy white eggs. Both parents share incubation, which lasts approximately 18–20 days, and both feed the chicks. The adults are aggressive in defence of the nest cavity, often chasing away much larger birds — including crows and raptors — with loud calls and dive-bombing attacks. The chicks fledge after approximately 28–30 days.
