About this Species

Appearance

The Indian Pitta (Pitta brachyura) is one of Asia's most spectacularly coloured songbirds, celebrated for its remarkable nine-tone plumage that has earned it the Sanskrit name navaranga. The upperparts are a rich turquoise-green with a bold buff-white supercilium running from the forehead to the nape. The crown and broad eye-stripe are deep black, while the cheeks and ear-coverts are a warm buff-brown. The underparts are a clean buffy-white on the breast, transitioning to a vivid scarlet-red vent and undertail. The wings show a striking turquoise-blue panel and a brilliant white wing-patch that flashes conspicuously in flight. The rump is an iridescent cobalt-blue.

Both sexes are very similar in plumage, making field identification by sex extremely difficult. The bill is strong, slightly decurved and dark greyish-black, well-suited to probing leaf litter and soft earth. The legs are stout and pinkish-buff, adapted for vigorous ground-hopping. The tail is very short — almost stub-like — giving the bird a distinctive, rotund silhouette quite unlike most other passerines.

Behaviour & Feeding

The Indian Pitta is a secretive, ground-dwelling bird that spends the majority of its time beneath the forest canopy, moving with quick hops through deep leaf litter. It forages by systematically tossing dead leaves aside with its bill to expose earthworms, beetles, termites and other invertebrates hidden in the moist soil beneath. Despite its brilliant colours, it is surprisingly difficult to spot in the dappled shadows of the forest floor; the bird is far more often heard than seen.

When disturbed it will often fly up to a low branch and sit motionless, relying on broken light and shadow to obscure its outline. At dusk and dawn it is considerably more active and vocal, delivering its piercing two-note whistle — a sound well-known to birdwatchers across the Indian subcontinent and Sri Lanka. In Sri Lanka the species is a winter visitor, arriving in October–November and departing by April, although a small number may remain longer.

Breeding

Breeding takes place in the foothills and lower mountain ranges of peninsular India, typically between May and August. The nest is a large, domed globe of grass, leaves and twigs with a side entrance, placed on or very near the ground — sometimes tucked against a boulder or tree root. The female lays four to six rounded white eggs with reddish-brown spots and blotches. Both parents share incubation duties, and the chicks are attended by both adults until fledging.
