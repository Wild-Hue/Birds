Sri Lanka Swallow

Cecropis hyperythra

Weight 14–18 g
Length 18–19 cm
Wingspan ~32 cm
Endemic · Sri Lanka

Sri Lanka's only endemic swallow — a graceful, richly coloured aerial hunter found nowhere else on earth. With its deep chestnut underparts, glossy steel-blue upperparts and long forked tail, it is one of the most beautiful members of the swallow family. It sweeps effortlessly over open landscapes, forest clearings and wetlands, catching insects on the wing with extraordinary agility.

Field Notes
🌾
Habitat
Open Country & Forest Edge
Paddy fields, open grasslands, forest clearings, wetland margins, river valleys and the vicinity of human habitation. Often seen near cliffs and buildings used for nesting.
📍
Distribution
Sri Lanka Only
Strictly endemic — found throughout Sri Lanka from the coast to mid-elevations, in both wet and dry zones. Most numerous in lowland open country.
⚖️
Weight & Size
14–18 g · 18–19 cm
Slightly larger and more robust than the Barn Swallow. The long outer tail feathers add considerably to its overall length and aerial elegance.
🦟
Diet
Aerial Insectivore
Catches flying insects — including flies, beetles, moths and winged ants — entirely on the wing during long, sweeping foraging flights.
🔊
Voice
Soft tswit-tswit
A gentle, twittering call given in flight — softer and less chatty than the Barn Swallow. Also produces a short, musical warbling song near the nest.
🟢
Conservation
Least Concern
IUCN Least Concern. Reasonably common throughout its endemic range. Adapts well to human-modified habitats; frequently nests on buildings and bridges.
About this Species

Appearance

The Sri Lanka Swallow (Cecropis hyperythra) is a medium-sized, strikingly coloured swallow endemic to Sri Lanka. The upperparts are a deep, glossy steel-blue — almost black in poor light — with a rich chestnut-rufous rump patch that is highly visible in flight. The forehead and cheeks are deep chestnut. The underparts are a warm, deep chestnut-orange, distinctly darker and richer than the pale underparts typical of most swallow species, giving this bird an unusually saturated, jewel-toned appearance.

The tail is prominently forked, with elongated outer streamers that accentuate the bird's aerial grace. The bill is small and flattened — typical of aerial insectivores — and the legs and feet are dark. Sexes are similar, though females may average slightly shorter outer tail feathers. Juveniles are duller and browner above, with a less deeply coloured rufous wash below.

Behaviour & Flight

The Sri Lanka Swallow is a consummate aerial acrobat, spending the majority of its active hours in the air. Its flight is fluid and graceful, combining long glides with rapid bursts of wingbeats as it pursues insects over open ground, water bodies and forest edges. It frequently forages low over paddy fields and wetlands, sometimes skimming the water surface.

Unlike many swallows, it is often seen perching openly on telegraph wires, bare branches and rooftops, where its richly coloured plumage is displayed to full advantage. It is generally found in pairs or small family groups outside the breeding season, occasionally gathering in larger loose flocks at favoured foraging sites or pre-roost gatherings.

Breeding

Nesting takes place on man-made structures as readily as natural sites — ledges under bridges, culverts, building eaves and rocky outcrops are all used. The nest is a cup of mud pellets, lined with fine grass and feathers, plastered to a vertical or overhanging surface. The clutch typically consists of three to four white eggs, often lightly spotted. Both parents share incubation and chick-rearing duties. Multiple broods may be raised in a season in favourable conditions.
