Malabar Pied Hornbill

Anthracoceros coronatus

Weight 600–900 g
Length 65–68 cm
Wingspan ~90 cm
Sri Lanka · India

One of South Asia's most striking forest birds — the Malabar Pied Hornbill commands attention with its bold black-and-white plumage and an enormous ivory-yellow casqued bill. A resident of tall tropical forests and well-wooded gardens, it is often heard before it is seen: loud, raucous calls and the deep whooshing of its wingbeats announce its arrival from considerable distance.

Field Notes
🌳
Habitat
Tall Forest & Groves
Primary and secondary tropical moist deciduous and evergreen forest, forest edges, well-wooded gardens and coconut plantations with large fruiting trees.
📍
Distribution
Sri Lanka · Peninsular India
Resident across Sri Lanka's wet zone and patches of the dry zone; also found across much of peninsular India and into the Terai foothills. Non-migratory.
⚖️
Weight & Size
600–900 g · 65–68 cm
A large, stocky hornbill. The bill and casque alone can account for nearly a third of the bird's total body length.
🍇
Diet
Frugivore / Omnivore
Primarily wild figs and other forest fruits; supplements diet with large insects, lizards, frogs, eggs and nestlings of smaller birds.
🔊
Voice
Loud kek-kek-kek
Raucous, far-carrying calls — a rapid yelping or cackling sequence. Wingbeats produce a loud, rhythmic whooshing sound audible from a distance.

IUCN Near Threatened. Declining due to deforestation, loss of large nesting trees and hunting pressure. Dependent on old-growth trees for nesting cavities.
About this Species

Appearance

The Malabar Pied Hornbill (Anthracoceros coronatus) is a large, dramatically patterned bird measuring 65–68 cm. The plumage is boldly black and white: the head, neck, breast, back and wings are glossy black with a green iridescence, while the belly, outer tail feathers and a prominent white trailing edge to the wings are white. The white on the tail and wing tips flashes conspicuously in flight, making the bird unmistakable even at a distance.

The most defining feature is the enormous yellow-ivory bill topped by a large, laterally flattened casque — a hollow structure formed from keratin. The casque of the male is larger and bears a black patch at its base; in the female the casque is smaller and the bare facial skin around the eye is bluish rather than pinkish. The eye is dark brown, and the legs are dark grey.

Behaviour & Feeding

Malabar Pied Hornbills are highly social birds, commonly seen in pairs or small noisy groups that move between fruiting trees. They are important seed dispersers for many large-seeded forest trees — particularly wild figs — and play a critical ecological role in maintaining forest regeneration. When foraging, they pick fruit directly from branches with the tip of the massive bill and toss it back to swallow.

Their flight is distinctive: alternating bursts of flapping with short glides, accompanied by the loud whoosh of air through the primary feathers. They roost communally, sometimes in large groups of dozens to hundreds, gathering at traditional roost sites each evening in a spectacular aerial display.

Breeding

Like all hornbills, the Malabar Pied Hornbill uses a sealed tree-cavity nest. The female enters a natural hollow in a large tree and seals herself inside with a mixture of mud, droppings and food remains — leaving only a narrow vertical slit through which the male delivers food. She remains sealed inside throughout incubation and the early nestling period, moulting all her flight feathers in the process. The male must provide all food for both the female and chicks during this time — a demanding commitment that can last several months.

The clutch is typically one to three white eggs. Nesting success is heavily dependent on the availability of large, old trees with suitable natural cavities — a resource that is increasingly scarce in degraded forest landscapes.
