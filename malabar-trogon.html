Malabar Trogon

Harpactes fasciatus

Weight 72–98 g
Length 29–31 cm
Wingspan ~43 cm
Sri Lanka · Western Ghats · India

A resplendent gem of the rainforest canopy — the Malabar Trogon is one of Asia's most visually striking birds. The male's crimson-red underparts and jet-black head glow against the dark forest interior, while its soft, mellow calls drift through the moist undergrowth of Sri Lanka's wet zone forests and India's Western Ghats.

Field Notes
🌿
Habitat
Moist Evergreen Forest
Dense, tall humid broadleaf and semi-evergreen forests with closed canopy. Favours shaded mid-storey perches in undisturbed wet zone forest and forest edges.
📍
Distribution
Sri Lanka & Western Ghats
Endemic to the Indian subcontinent. Resident in Sri Lanka's wet zone, Western Ghats (India), parts of the Eastern Ghats and the hill forests of central India.
⚖️
Weight & Size
72–98 g · 29–31 cm
Stocky, broad-tailed bird with a relatively large head and short rounded wings. The long, square-tipped tail is distinctive even in silhouette against the forest canopy.
🍃
Diet
Insects, Fruits & Lizards
Primarily large insects such as beetles, mantids and caterpillars, supplemented with small lizards and wild berries. Sallies from a perch to snatch prey from foliage or mid-air.
🔊
Voice
Soft kow-kow-kow
A slow, mellow series of descending "kow kow kow" notes — plaintive and far-carrying through dense forest. Also produces a rapid, churring alarm call when disturbed.
🟢
Conservation
Least Concern
IUCN Red List — Least Concern. Stable across its range, though locally vulnerable to deforestation and habitat fragmentation in the Western Ghats and Sri Lanka's wet zone.
About this Species

Appearance

The Malabar Trogon (Harpactes fasciatus) is one of the most visually arresting birds of the South Asian rainforest. The male is unmistakable — his jet-black head, throat and upper breast contrast sharply with a narrow white crescentic breast-band that separates the dark hood from the vivid crimson-red lower breast and belly. The upperparts — back, scapulars and wing-coverts — are a rich cinnamon-brown, finely vermiculated with black. The rump and uppertail-coverts are also dark crimson. The wings show a finely barred black-and-white pattern on the inner coverts. The long, graduated tail is dark above and white below on the outer feathers, creating a striking pattern during flight and display.

The female is considerably more subdued: her head and breast are olive-brown to warm tawny-brown, the underparts are a paler orange-buff, and she lacks the bold black-and-red tones of the male. Both sexes share the characteristic broad, flat bill — an adaptation for catching large insects — which is yellow at the base and pale greyish at the tip, surrounded by prominent rictal bristles. The eye is encircled by a conspicuous bare blue orbital ring, a feature shared with many trogons and particularly vivid in the male.

Behaviour & Feeding

The Malabar Trogon is a lethargic, upright-perching bird that sits motionless for long periods on a mid-storey branch, scanning the surrounding foliage for prey. When a suitable insect or small lizard is spotted, it makes a brief, swift sally — either snatching the prey directly from a leaf or catching it in mid-air — before returning to its perch. This sit-and-wait hunting strategy, combined with its tendency to remain in the dense shaded interior of the forest, means it is frequently overlooked despite its brilliant colours. It is far more often detected by voice than by sight.

In Sri Lanka, the Malabar Trogon is a resident bird of the wet zone lowlands and lower hills, found in mature rainforest and well-wooded plantations. It is one of the flagship species of Sri Lanka's endemic-rich rainforests at Sinharaja, Kitulgala and Kanneliya. It occasionally supplements its insect diet with small wild fruits, particularly figs, and has been observed following army ant columns to snap up flushed invertebrates.

Breeding

Breeding in Sri Lanka occurs mainly between February and May, coinciding with the drier inter-monsoon period. Unlike many birds, trogons nest in natural tree cavities or self-excavated holes in rotten wood — sometimes even in active termite mounds. The cavity entrance is small and rounded, and the nest chamber is lined sparsely with plant fibres and feathers. The female lays two to four pale cream or pinkish-white eggs. Both parents share incubation, which lasts approximately 17–19 days, and both attend the chicks until fledging. The male's striking plumage fades slightly during the breeding season as a result of feather wear.
