About this Species

Appearance

The Small Minivet (Pericrocotus cinnamomeus) is the smallest member of its genus, measuring just 15–16 cm and weighing 6–12 g. The male is immediately recognisable by his slate-grey crown, cheeks and back, set against vivid orange-red underparts, rump and wing patches. The face, lore and upper throat are blackish-grey, giving the impression of a dark mask. The belly gradually fades to a pale yellowish-white, and the tail is black with orange edges.

Females are considerably more subdued — light grey above with pale yellow to creamy-white underparts, dark grey wings and a yellowish rump and wing patch. Both sexes share a strong, pointed dark-grey bill and black legs and feet. There is notable subspecific variation across the range: the northwestern form P. c. pallidus is much paler overall, while the southern Indian P. c. malabaricus is darker above with more extensive scarlet below.

Behaviour & Feeding

Small Minivets are almost always found in small, active flocks that move restlessly through the mid- and upper canopy. They are agile insectivores, gleaning insects and small arthropods directly from leaf surfaces, bark and twigs, and frequently making short aerial sallies to catch prey in flight — much like flycatchers. Their preferred habitats include deciduous forest edges, thorn jungle, scrub, plantations, parks and gardens; they can even turn up in suburban areas.

The species is highly vocal while foraging. The call is a constant, high-pitched swee-swee-swee or tswee-eet — a thin, musical whistle that often betrays the presence of an approaching flock long before the birds themselves come into view. In Sri Lanka, the Small Minivet is a widespread lowland resident of the dry and intermediate zones, frequently associating with mixed-species foraging parties.

Breeding

The nest is a compact, cup-shaped structure built from fine twigs, grass and plant fibres, bound together with spider silk and placed in a horizontal fork of a tree — often well above the ground. The female lays two to four spotted eggs and handles most of the incubation. Both parents take part in raising the chicks.
