Loten's Sunbird

Cinnyris lotenius

Weight 8–12 g
Length 12–13 cm
Wingspan ~16 cm
Sri Lanka · South India

A jewel-bright resident of Sri Lanka's gardens and forest edges — Loten's Sunbird is distinguished from its cousin the Purple Sunbird by its exceptionally long, strongly decurved bill and the male's flashing maroon-red pectoral tufts. A restless, aggressive nectar feeder and important pollinator, it is one of the island's most familiar garden birds.

Field Notes
🌿
Habitat
Gardens, Forest Edges & Scrub
Home gardens, flowering trees, scrub jungle, forest clearings and edges, coconut and rubber plantations, and urban parks. Highly adaptable — found from coastal scrub to mid-hill forest edges up to about 1,200 m elevation.
📍
Distribution
Sri Lanka & South India
Resident across the entire island of Sri Lanka from sea level to the mid-hills — widespread in both wet and dry zones. Also found along the south-western coast of peninsular India from Goa southward. Non-migratory.
⚖️
Weight & Size
8–12 g · 12–13 cm
Slender and compact with an exceptionally long, strongly decurved bill — noticeably longer than that of the similar Purple Sunbird. This elongated bill is the single most reliable field character separating the two species.
🍃
Diet
Nectar, Insects & Spiders
Primarily flower nectar, taken by hovering or clinging. Supplements diet with small insects, spiders and occasionally tiny fruit pulp — providing essential protein, especially during the breeding season and when feeding chicks.
🔊
Voice
Loud chweet · chee-chee-chee
A sharp, loud "chweet" contact call and a fast, high-pitched warbling song — "chee-chee-chee-chwee-chwit". Males sing persistently from an exposed perch, especially in the early morning and during territorial disputes.
🟢
Conservation
Least Concern
IUCN Red List — Least Concern. Common and widespread across its relatively restricted range. Adapts well to human-modified habitats and benefits greatly from garden plantings of flowering trees and shrubs.
About this Species

Appearance

The Loten's Sunbird (Cinnyris lotenius) is a small, slender nectarivore most immediately distinguished from all other Sri Lankan sunbirds by its exceptionally long, strongly decurved bill — proportionally the longest bill of any sunbird in Sri Lanka, curving downward in a deep arc that gives the bird a distinctive, almost sickle-billed profile. The breeding male is a strikingly handsome bird: the entire head, throat, upper breast, mantle, back and wing-coverts are a deep, glossy metallic purple-black that shifts between purple, green and blue depending on the angle of light — an iridescence rivalling that of a hummingbird. The belly and flanks are a dark, sooty blackish-brown, contrasting subtly with the glossy upperparts.

The male's most spectacular feature is a pair of concealed pectoral tufts — vivid maroon-red to scarlet-orange — tucked beneath the wings on either side of the breast. These tufts are normally hidden but are displayed prominently during courtship and aggressive encounters, fanning out against the dark breast like burning embers. A narrow, dull yellow pectoral band borders the lower edge of the glossy breast in some individuals. The female is considerably plainer: olive-brown above with a pale supercilium, and yellowish-white to pale olive below, with faint streaking on the breast. She too has the characteristically long bill — the most reliable character for distinguishing her from the female Purple Sunbird in the field. Eclipse males resemble females but retain patches of glossy plumage on the throat and crown.

Behaviour & Feeding

Loten's Sunbird is a fast-moving, pugnacious and highly territorial nectar feeder. The male defends flowering trees and shrubs aggressively against other sunbirds — including his own species — chasing intruders in rapid aerial pursuits accompanied by loud alarm calls. It feeds primarily by hovering briefly or clinging acrobatically to flowers to insert its long bill deep into the corolla and lap nectar with its brush-tipped, tubular tongue. However, unlike many sunbirds, Loten's also regularly pierces the base of flowers from the outside — particularly those with long corollas such as Hibiscus and Ixora — to rob nectar without pollinating the flower, making it simultaneously a legitimate pollinator and a nectar robber depending on the flower type.

In Sri Lanka it is one of the most important pollinators of garden flowering plants, visiting a wide range of species including Ixora, Heliconia, Callistemon, Salvia, Tecoma, papaya flowers and many wild forest blossoms. It is abundant and conspicuous across the entire island and is a familiar garden bird in Colombo, Kandy, Galle and virtually every populated area of the country. The species is non-migratory and pairs occupy stable home territories year-round, the male singing persistently from prominent exposed perches at the top of flowering shrubs.

Breeding

Loten's Sunbird breeds throughout much of the year in Sri Lanka, with peaks between January and May and again in August–September, broadly corresponding to drier periods when flowering is most prolific. The nest is a beautifully crafted, pear-shaped pendant purse — one of the finest constructions of any Sri Lankan passerine. It is woven from fine grass, plant fibres, hair and spider silk, suspended by a long thread from the tip of a thin hanging branch or the underside of a broad leaf, typically 2–5 metres above the ground. The entrance is on the side near the top, with a small porch or hood of woven material projecting above it. The outside is decorated with lichen, bark fragments and plant debris bound with cobweb, providing excellent camouflage. The female alone builds the nest and incubates the clutch of two pale greenish-white eggs spotted with grey and brown. Incubation lasts approximately 15–17 days. Both parents feed the chicks, which fledge at around 17–19 days.
